DCO₂ — the relationship most learners get backwards

DCO₂ = VT² × Frequency
↑ Amplitude → ↑ VT (squared effect) → ↑ DCO₂ → ↓ PaCO₂
↑ Frequency alone → ↓ VT (less time to deliver each oscillation) → DCO₂ often falls despite the higher number.

This relationship assumes amplitude stays unchanged. In clinical practice, amplitude is often increased alongside frequency specifically to maintain tidal volume — don't over-generalise "higher frequency clears less CO₂" to every real scenario.

Lung recruitment curve — the open-lung strategy

This patient's collapsed alveoli tend to open above the green line and re-collapse below the red line as severity changes — the gap between them is hysteresis. The open-lung strategy sets MAP just above the closing pressure, on the high-compliance plateau, rather than chasing the peak.

Pattern reference

A-lines — horizontal reverberation artefacts below the pleura; normal, well-aerated lung.
B-lines — vertical comet-tails from the pleura; interstitial fluid/oedema, an early sign of de-recruitment.
White lung — coalescent B-lines with no discrete A-lines left; severe interstitial syndrome.
Consolidation — hepatised, tissue-like lung; air bronchograms are dynamic if the airway is patent, static if obstructed.

    Core formulas

    MAP = ΔP × (Ti / (Ti+Te)) + PEEP

    Mean airway pressure rises with higher PIP, longer Ti, or higher PEEP — and drives oxygenation more than PaCO₂.

    DCO₂ = VT² × Frequency

    In HFOV, CO₂ clearance depends far more on tidal volume than on frequency, because of the squared term.

    τ = R × C

    The lung's time constant. Three time constants (≈95% of exhalation) must fit inside Te, or gas trapping (auto-PEEP) results.

    Target VT ≈ 4–6 mL/kg

    This app defaults Volume Guarantee to 5 mL/kg, adjustable per patient and disease state.

    Target VT by diagnosis

    DiagnosisSuggested VTWhy
    RDS4–5 mL/kgSmall, stiff, surfactant-deficient lungs — lower volumes limit volutrauma while compliance is worst.
    Normal lungs4–6 mL/kgStandard physiological range for a structurally normal neonatal lung.
    BPD5–7 mL/kgHigher dead space and non-uniform time constants often need a larger volume to achieve adequate minute ventilation.
    Pulmonary hypoplasia4–5 mL/kgSmall lung volume overall — higher VT targets risk overdistending the limited alveolar surface available.
    MAS5–6 mL/kgPatchy atelectasis alongside air trapping — mid-range volumes balance recruitment against gas trapping risk.
